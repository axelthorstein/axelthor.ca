boiling point

I come home and my big sister removes my little shoes,

I follow her into the kitchen and see the kettle has been left alone too long.

Billows of steam surge from the spout, and

The steadily growing whistle burrows deeper in our ears.

She moves to lift the kettle, but to no avail.

I watch confusion and frustration build in her eyes

As she teaches herself to manage the situation.

Our father has worked 86 of the last 72 hours,

He cannot help her, the kettle only boils more.

I come home and remove my mitts

Pulling the string that connects them through the sleeves.

Then I pull off my bright blue winter boots

And place them in a puddle with the other shoes.

I walk into the kitchen and check if the kettle is boiling,

I can hear the crackling of the metal, but it has yet to whistle.

I reach up for the handle, but I am still too little,

I cannot reach. It is out of my control.

My older sister walks past me, and

With her practiced hands removes the kettle from the element,

Today will be quiet.

I come home and remove my backpack and rubber boots.

Today the kettle will boil, so my sister takes me into the kitchen.

She places her hands on mine and leads them to the handle.

“I know you are little, but one day I will leave,

And you will bare of the kettle for your sisters as I have done for you.”

I am too weak to lift the kettle alone, but together we are able.

“Don’t be angry little brother, this is our reality and you must use it to build strength.”

I come home and remove my baseball cap and sneakers,

Since she left I have grown stronger, and cold.

I walk into the kitchen and find the kettle simmering,

I lift the kettle off the element and place it onto our cool counter.

I sit at our table with my younger sisters to an endless game of olsen olsen.

I catch a glimpse of of the stove; the kettle has moved back onto the element,

It has resumed boiling.

I will not be party to this cycle today.

I will let it boil.

I come home and remove my blazer and brogues.

My little sister meets me at the door and leads me to the kitchen.

I can hear the faint crackle and whistle that signals the building pressure.

I take her hands and lead them to the handle,

And together we move the kettle off the element.

She is young, but one day I will leave, and

She too will need to learn this strength,

As one day she will teach her younger sister.

Now I pass on the responsibility, as my sister before me.

We will always share this burden together,

Each carrying one quarter of the emotional load.

Each hardened like the wave battered stones,

Always anticipating, but never knowing the magnitude of the crash,

Little by little being shaped by it,

But over time leaving only the strongest layers.

- axel thor
